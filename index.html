01 — Introduction

The Legendary Tournament

Every four years, the world stops to watch a single tournament.

It began in 1930, when thirteen nations sailed to Uruguay for a competition no one was sure would last. Ninety years later, the FIFA World Cup is the most watched event on earth, serving as the most challenging test for the best footballers. This is its story, told through data: how the tournament grew, the nations that host and win it, Australia's place in it, the players who defined it, and the road to a bigger World Cup in 2026.

Lionel Messi, 2022 World Cup

02 — How it grew

The History of the World Cup

What began in 1930 as a modest gathering of thirteen nations has grown into a truly global contest, with teams from every continent. Each tournament has pulled in more countries, more players, and bigger crowds than the last, as shown in the map below.

A Growing Global Game

The map becomes more colorful as the slider shifts from 1930 to 2022; a competition that began among a small group of countries gradually spreads to every continent. A country's rate of appearance increases with the depth of the shade. As the only country to participate in all 22 World Cups, Brazil stands as the top participant. Argentina, Italy, and Germany, the traditional heavyweights that have shaped the tournament's history, are next.

How to read: Drag the slider to choose a year. Each country is shaded by how many World Cups it had reached up to that point. The deeper the red, the more appearances.

Crowds Through the Decades

Each row compares the average attendance of a tournament with its peak attendance record. The most spectacular was in 1950, when 173,850 fans flocked to Rio's Maracanã for the final match between Uruguay and Brazil. This attendance remains the biggest in World Cup history. The tournament's appeal has only increased as it has expanded around the globe, as evidenced by the rise in average attendance, which peaked when the United States hosted in 1994.

How to read: Each row is one tournament. The blue ball marks the average crowd per match and the red ball marks the single biggest crowd. The longer the green line, the bigger the gap between a typical match and the record.

03 — Geography

Countries of the Tournament

The World Cup has always belonged to the whole planet, but only a handful of nations have shaped it from the centre. Some have hosted the tournament and welcomed the world to their stadiums, a smaller few have gone on to lift the trophy itself. This map brings both stories together.

Hosts and Champions

Hosting duties have spread steadily outward, from South America to other continents of the world. Despite this, winning has stayed far more concentrated, where only 8 nations have won the World Cup.

04 — Closer to home

Australia at the World Cup

The Socceroos

Australia's men have reached the World Cup six times, but their story is one of narrow margins and rare breakthroughs. The Socceroos' finest hour came in 2006, when a golden generation reached the knockout rounds for the first time before a heartbreaking late exit to Italy. Most campaigns, though, have ended at the group stage.

How to read: The heatmap shows every match by tournament. Green is a win, grey is a draw, red is a loss. The radar shows Australia's top three Socceroos scorers across five measures. The further from the centre, the higher the value.

Tim Cahill, 2018 World Cup

The Matildas

The Matildas have become the face of Australian women’s football. Since their 1995 debut they have grown from early exits into genuine contenders, peaking on home soil in 2023 when they reached the semi-finals and finished fourth, which is the best World Cup result by any senior Australian side, men's or women's.

How to read: The heatmap shows every match by tournament. Green is a win, grey is a draw, red is a loss. The radar profiles their top three Matildas scorers across five measures. The further from the centre, the higher the value.

Sam Kerr, 2023 Women's World Cup

05 — The legends

Iconic Teams & Players

Australia's story is one of many woven into the World Cup, but some names tower over them all. Across the tournament's history, certain teams and players have become legends, turning football's greatest stage into the place where their names were made. These are a few of the national teams and individuals who defined it.

Brazil: The nation with the most World Cup wins

No nation embodies the World Cup like Brazil. Five World Cups won, more than anyone, and the only country to play in all twenty-two tournaments. From Pelé's 1958 breakthrough to the samba football of 1970 and the steel of 1994 and 2002, Brazil has turned winning into an art form.

How to read: Each ribbon flows from a champion nation to the year it won. The more bands there are, the more the nation has won.

Brazil's victory, 2002 World Cup

Pelé: The player with the most World Cup wins

Pelé is the only player to win the World Cup three times, which is in 1958, 1962, and 1970. He burst onto the scene as a seventeen year old in Sweden and left as the tournament's defining figure, scoring in finals across three different decades.

How to read: Each player has three bars: World Cup wins, tournaments played, and career World Cup goals. Pelé is the only one that has three World Cup wins.

Pelé, 1970 World Cup

Miroslav Klose: The player with the most World Cup goals

No one has scored more World Cup goals than Miroslav Klose. Across four tournaments from 2002 to 2014, the German striker netted sixteen times, overtaking Ronaldo Nazário's record on his way to lifting the trophy in 2014.

How to read: The bars rank the all-time top World Cup scorers. Drag the slider through the years to watch Klose climb to the top.

Miroslav Klose, 2014 World Cup

Diego Forlán: A redemption story

Diego Forlán was known as a flop, yet during the 2010 World Cup, he pulled off one of the most shocking performances. Forlán and the Jabulani ball was like a love story: when no one could control it, he scored five times, making him one of the top scorers and finishing as the World Cup's best player.

How to read: Each bar tracks a player's goals round by round at the 2010 World Cup. Drag the slider to follow Forlán's rise to joint-top scorer.

Diego Forlán, 2010 World Cup

06 — What's next

The Road to 2026

The Last Dance

While young talents like Lamine Yamal will play in their first World Cup, many of the sport's greatest footballers, including Lionel Messi, Cristiano Ronaldo, and Neymar Jr are expected to play their final tournament. As a fitting send-off, 2026 has been planned as the biggest World Cup ever: 48 teams across three host nations, the United States, Mexico, and Canada.
